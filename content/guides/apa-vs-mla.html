Key Differences Between APA and MLA

APA and MLA are the two most common academic citation styles, but they serve different purposes and follow distinct rules. Understanding these differences is essential for academic success:

APA Style (American Psychological Association)

  • Field of Use: Primarily used in social sciences (psychology, sociology, education, business)
  • Focus: Emphasizes date of publication to highlight research currency
  • Paper Structure: Requires title page, abstract, body, references
  • In-Text Citation: Author-date format (Smith, 2020)
  • Reference Page: Titled "References" with hanging indents
  • Headings: Uses up to 5 hierarchical heading levels

MLA Style (Modern Language Association)

    极Field of Use: Humanities-focused (literature, philosophy, arts, languages)
  • Focus: Highlights authorship and source titles
  • Paper Structure: No title page (unless requested), header with name/page, Works Cited
  • In-Text Citation: Author-page format (Smith 24)
  • Reference Page: Titled "Works Cited" with hanging indents
  • Headings: Simpler structure with consistent formatting

Core Distinction: APA prioritizes research date (important in sciences), while MLA emphasizes authorship (crucial in humanities). This difference reflects their distinct academic purposes.

APA vs MLA Formatting Rules Comparison

The formatting differences between APA and MLA extend beyond citations. This table summarizes key formatting rules:

Formatting Element APA 7th Edition MLA 9th Edition
Title Page Required with title, author, affiliation Not required (unless specified)
Header Running head (short title) + page number Last name + page number
Margins 1 inch on all sides 1 inch on all sides
Font Times New Roman 12pt or similar Times New Roman 12pt
Spacing Double-spaced throughout Double-spaced throughout
In-Text Citation (Author, Year, p. Page) or (Author, Year) (Author Page) or (Page)
Multiple Authors 3+ authors: (First et al., Year) 3+ authors: (First et al. Page)
Reference List "References" page, alphabetical "Works Cited" page, alphabetical
DOI/URL Format Include DOI as hyperlink Include stable URL or permalink

When to Use APA vs MLA

Choosing the right citation style depends on your discipline and assignment requirements:

When to Use APA Style

  • Psychology & Social Sciences: Research papers, case studies, literature reviews
  • Education & Nursing: Evidence-based practice papersBusiness & Economics: Market analyses, business reports
  • Scientific Research: When emphasizing recent studies is crucial
  • Quantitative Studies: Papers with statistical analysis

When to Use MLA Style

  • Literature & Languages: Literary analyses, poetry interpretations
  • Philosophy & Religion: Theoretical papers, textual analysis
  • Cultural Studies: Film, media, and art criticism
  • History & Anthropology: Historical analysis papers
  • Qualitative Research: When authorship is more important than date

Professional Advice: When uncertain, consult your instructor or publication guidelines. Some interdisciplinary fields accept both styles, but generally consistency within a discipline is critical.

APA vs MLA FAQ

Can I use APA for humanities or MLA for sciences? +

While possible, it's generally not recommended. APA is designed for scientific writing where research dates matter, while MLA better serves humanities where authorship and source titles are paramount. Using the wrong style may confuse readers familiar with disciplinary conventions.

Which style is easier to learn: APA or MLA? +

MLA is generally considered simpler for beginners due to fewer formatting requirements (no abstract or running head needed). However, APA offers more structured guidelines that some students find clearer. Most writing centers report students master MLA faster initially.

Do APA and MLA require different fonts or spacing? +

Both styles require:

  • 12-point Times New Roman font (or similar readable font)
  • Double-spacing throughout the document
  • 1-inch margins on all sides

The main differences lie in citation format and structural elements.

How do I cite websites in APA vs MLA? +

APA: Author. (Year, Month Day). Title of page. Site Name. URL

MLA: Author. "Title of Page." Site Name, Publisher, Date, URL.

APA emphasizes publication date, while MLA includes publisher information when available.

  • APA requires title page and abstract | MLA starts with content
